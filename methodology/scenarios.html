Implementation Scenarios

Diagnostic reasoning about why evidence-based strategies fail

Overview

Evidence-based teaching strategies sometimes fail in practice—not because the research is wrong, but because implementation conditions matter. The Implementation Scenarios benchmark tests whether AI systems can diagnose why a proven strategy isn't working in a specific context.

12
Scenarios
0-3
Points Per Item
36
Maximum Score

Rationale

Teachers and education policymakers often encounter situations where recommended practices don't produce expected results. The ability to diagnose implementation failures requires:

  • Understanding the mechanism behind a strategy (not just that it "works")
  • Recognising specific conditions that enable or inhibit effectiveness
  • Distinguishing between strategy failure and implementation failure

This benchmark tests whether AI can demonstrate this diagnostic reasoning, rather than simply recommending "evidence-based" approaches.

Scenario Topics

The 12 scenarios cover major evidence-based strategies:

ID Topic Implementation Failure
S01 Retrieval Practice Testing before encoding; no feedback provided
S02 Interleaving Switching between unrelated topics without connection
S03 Worked Examples Providing examples without fading or practice
S04 Spaced Practice Spacing without initial mastery
S05 Formative Assessment Assessment without responsive instruction
S06 Cognitive Load Reducing load for experts (expertise reversal effect)
S07 Direct Instruction Lecture without active engagement
S08 Critical Thinking Teaching "skills" without domain knowledge
S09 Feedback Timing Immediate feedback on complex tasks
S10 Scaffolding Permanent scaffolds that prevent independence
S11 Rewards (Overjustification) Extrinsic rewards undermining intrinsic motivation
S12 Personalisation Surface personalisation without adaptation

Scenario Structure

Each scenario presents:

  1. A classroom context with specific constraints
  2. A teacher implementing an evidence-based strategy
  3. Observed outcomes that fall short of expectations

The model must explain why the strategy isn't working in this specific context.

Example Scenario (S01: Retrieval Practice)

A Year 8 science teacher has read about the benefits of retrieval practice for long-term retention. At the start of each lesson, she gives students a quiz on the topic they're about to learn. Students struggle with the questions and often guess. After revealing the answers, she moves directly into teaching the new content. After several weeks, she notices that students still seem to forget the material quickly. A colleague suggests that retrieval practice "doesn't work for science." What is actually going wrong with how retrieval practice is being implemented here?

Scoring Rubric

Responses are scored on a 0-3 scale based on diagnostic accuracy:

Score Criteria Example
0 Misdiagnosis: Concludes the strategy is fundamentally flawed or suggests abandoning it "Retrieval practice may not be suitable for science content"
1 Generic: Correct direction but no specific mechanism identified "The implementation could be improved"
2 Partial: Identifies the core issue but misses important nuances "Testing before teaching violates retrieval practice principles"
3 Full: Accurate diagnosis with mechanism and terminology "Two critical issues: (1) testing before encoding violates the retrieval-must-follow-encoding sequence, (2) moving on without feedback eliminates the error correction mechanism that makes retrieval practice effective"

Scoring Process

Responses are evaluated by an LLM judge (Claude 4.5 Sonnet) using a structured rubric with examples. The judge provides:

  • A numerical score (0-3)
  • Reasoning explaining the score
  • Identification of key mechanisms mentioned or missed

A sample of responses is manually validated to ensure scoring consistency.

Key Findings

Implementation Scenarios reveal significant variation in diagnostic reasoning:

  • Top models achieve near-perfect scores (100% on some scenarios)
  • Weaker models often provide generic advice rather than specific diagnosis
  • Some scenarios (e.g., cognitive load, expertise reversal) prove consistently challenging
